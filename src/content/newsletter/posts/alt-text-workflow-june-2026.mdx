<p>You post a flyer. The date and price live inside the graphic. The caption says "new offer!" The alt field says <code>image1234</code> — or nothing at all.</p>
<p>Someone on a slow connection sees a broken image icon. Someone using a screen reader hears "image" and nothing else. Someone watching muted in a waiting room cannot select the text baked into your JPEG. The message did not land. The format left them out.</p>
<p>Images are <strong>content</strong>, not decoration. When they disappear — slow connection, screen reader, sensory overload, or a brain that processes text better than visuals — the information should still be there. <a href="https://www.pythonessprogrammer.com/neuroinclusive-design?utm_source=newsletter.pythonessprogrammer.com&amp;utm_medium=referral&amp;utm_campaign=alt-text-workflow-june-2026">Neuroinclusive design</a> calls this offering information in more than one format so different brains can enter. Alt text is how you make that true without rebuilding your entire visual brand.</p>
<p>The goal is a <strong>workflow</strong>, not a perfectionism spiral. Fifteen minutes today. A Sunday batch if you post often. Describe what matters, skip what does not.</p>
<p>If you have been following this month's accessible-design habits, you already know readable contrast and scannable structure change how your work feels — for strangers and for future you. This week we turn to the images themselves.</p>
<p>Pope Leo XIV's recent encyclical <a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html"><em>Magnifica Humanitas</em></a> charges that AI must be "<strong>disarmed, welcoming and accessible</strong>." Alt text is accessible in the most literal, hands-on sense: care for the human on the other end of the screen. Leo calls prudence about technology "<strong>responsible care for the human family</strong>." Describing your images honestly is a small act in that tradition — human care for other humans, not a checkbox.</p>

<h2>Part 1: If Images Disappeared, Would the Message Still Land?</h2>
<p><strong>The check:</strong> If images disappeared, would key information still be there?</p>
<p>Ask whether someone would still understand what you were offering, teaching, or selling — regardless of whether a compliance audit would pass.</p>
<p>Text baked into images (quotes, pricing, event dates) is the most common failure. The graphic looks great. The caption says "new offer!" without repeating the price. The alt field says <code>image1234</code> or stays empty. The visual channel failed. The caption failed. Assistive tech had nothing to read.</p>
<p><strong>Visual clutter drains everyone.</strong> Busy hero sections, autoplaying sliders, and six-font collages exhaust anyone scanning on a phone — not only neurodivergent readers. Alt text will not fix clutter; it pairs with clear structure and honest editing. This week we focus on <strong>describing what you keep.</strong></p>
<h2>Part 2: Why Alt Text Matters — For Humans, Search, and the Bots in Between</h2>
<p>Most people hear "alt text" and think <strong>screen readers.</strong> That alone would be reason enough. Screen readers and other assistive tech read alt text aloud or convert it to braille. If your flyer's date, price, or CTA lives only inside a JPEG, you have excluded anyone who cannot see the image — full stop.</p>
<p>The same description also helps the systems that increasingly shape whether your work is <strong>found</strong> at all.</p>
<p><strong>Search engines</strong> cannot "see" images the way humans do. They rely on alt text, filenames, and surrounding context. Descriptive alt text helps image search and page relevance — especially for product photos, portfolio work, and blog heroes. Skip the keyword stuffing. Write for clarity.</p>
<p><strong>AI agents</strong> are the newer lane — crawlers, summarizers, shopping assistants, and the tools that answer questions <em>about</em> your business without sending traffic to your site. <strong>AEO</strong> (Agent Engine Optimization) means making your content legible to those agents. They read structured text — headings, alt text, descriptive links — to infer what a page <em>means</em>. Vague or empty alt fields are the visual equivalent of a wall of text: the agent guesses, or skips you.</p>
<p>Leo XIV writes that <a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html">technology is never neutral</a> — it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, and use it. Communication works the same way. An image without description is a choice about who counts as your audience.</p>
<p>Clear headings plus descriptive alt text compound. Search engines and agents use both to build a model of your page. Accessible structure and accessible images reinforce each other.</p>
<p><strong>How to write good alt text (rules of thumb)</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><p><strong>Describe what matters for <em>this</em> context</strong> — not every pixel. "Woman in blue sweater" is thin; "Workshop facilitator demonstrating contrast check on laptop — June Accessible Tech session" is useful.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Include text that appears in the image</strong> — quotes, dates, prices, event titles. If it is critical, repeat it in the caption too (many platforms strip or hide alt).</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Skip "image of" / "photo of"</strong> — assistive tech already announces it is an image.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Decorative images:</strong> use empty alt (<code>alt=""</code>) so screen readers skip them. Not every visual needs a novel.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Functional images</strong> (icons, buttons): say what they <em>do</em> — "Search," "Download checklist PDF," not "magnifying glass."</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Keep it under ~125 characters when you can</strong> — some platforms truncate; put the critical noun first.</p></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Alt text template (copy this)</strong></p>

</code></pre>
<p><strong>Examples</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><p>Bad: <code>logo_final_v3.png</code></p></li>

</ul>
<p><strong>Your 15-minute action:</strong> Fix alt text on your <strong>top three</strong> images by traffic or importance. Set a recurring <strong>Sunday batch</strong> (15 minutes, five images) if you post often — same energy as a weekly inbox reset, but for visuals.</p>

<p><strong>A personal favorite: Bluesky's "require alt text" setting</strong></p>
<p>One of my favorite features on Bluesky lives in <strong>Settings → Accessibility</strong>. Turn on <strong>Require alt text before posting</strong>, and the app will not let you publish an image until you have written something in the alt field. No heroic willpower required — the platform holds the line for you.</p>
<p>I also keep <strong>Display larger alt text badges</strong> on, so I can see at a glance which posts in my feed actually have descriptions.</p>

<p>This matters beyond screen readers. On Bluesky, many news outlets, reporters, and activists simply <strong>will not repost</strong> content that ships without alt text. Your graphic might be brilliant. If the description field is empty, your reach stops at your own followers — not because the algorithm punished you, because thoughtful people refuse to amplify inaccessible posts.</p>
<p>If you post on Bluesky even occasionally, flip the setting on this week. Write one sentence per image. Watch how fast it becomes habit when skip is not an option.</p>
<h2>Part 4: A Harder Truth — "Ethical AI" and Who Gets to Define It</h2>
<p><a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html"><em>Magnifica Humanitas</em></a> landed in a complicated room. <a href="https://www.newyorker.com/news/the-lede/what-pope-leo-xiv-said-about-ai">Jill Lepore reports</a> that Anthropic co-founder <strong>Chris Olah</strong> appeared on the dais beside Leo at the release — unprecedented, and freighted with symbolism. Leo urges the public to <strong>push back</strong> against executives who resist restrictions. Olah's presence reminded me how easily "safety-oriented" and "ethical" get marketed while the underlying systems stay unchanged.</p>
<p>Leo writes that <a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html">a more moral AI is not enough if that morality is determined by a few</a>. I agree. We do not have "ethical AI" in any finished sense — not from Anthropic, not from anyone. We have products, incentives, and pattern matchers.</p>
<p>Karen Hao's <a href="https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/743569/empire-of-ai-by-karen-hao/"><em>Empire of AI</em></a> sharpened that for me:</p>
<blockquote><p>"In fact, deep learning models are inherently prone to having discriminatory impacts because they pick up and amplify even the tiniest imbalances present in huge volumes of training data. It's not just a problem when a demographic is poorly represented, but when it's overrepresented as well."</p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>"No matter their scale, neural networks are still statistical pattern matchers. And those patterns are still at times faulty or irrelevant, now just more intricate and more inscrutable than ever."</p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>"Even the term <em>hallucinations</em> is subtly misleading. It suggests that the bad behavior is an aberration, a bug, when it's a feature of the probabilistic pattern-matching mechanics of neural networks."</p></blockquote>
<p>So where does that leave <strong>you</strong>, writing alt text on a Sunday batch? In the gap between systems you cannot fix alone and surfaces you <strong>can</strong>. Alt text disarms nothing at Big Tech scale. On your own pages and posts, it makes meaning available to humans and to the automated intermediaries between you and your audience. That is the accessible, welcoming lane at human scale.</p>
<h2>Part 5: Someone on the Other End — Text in the Image Only</h2>
<p>A follower sees your Story: beautiful typography, hard-to-read contrast, a caption with no structure, date and link <strong>only</strong> inside the graphic. They are watching muted in a waiting room. They cannot hear you. They cannot select the text in the image.</p>
<p>They tap away. The format left them out.</p>
<p>Batch alt text and caption discipline are how you host people who are not experiencing your content in the ideal conditions you had when you designed it.</p>
<p><strong>Reflection prompt:</strong> Which recurring image type do you post most (quote card, flyer, product flat-lay)? That type gets the template first.</p>
