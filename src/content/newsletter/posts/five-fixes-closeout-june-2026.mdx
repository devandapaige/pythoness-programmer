<h2>Part 1: Fix 4 — Video &amp; Audio That Include</h2>
<p><strong>The check:</strong> Can someone get the message without sound or with low attention?</p>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Lens</th><th>Pick one surface this week</th><th>What to change</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Small business</strong></td><td>Sales video, tutorial, Zoom replay, podcast episode page</td><td>Captions on; transcript or summary linked nearby</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Chronically online</strong></td><td>Reel, TikTok, live replay, audio clip</td><td>Auto-captions <strong>reviewed</strong> (they are not perfect); critical info duplicated in caption or pinned comment</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>Your 15-minute action:</strong> On your <strong>last published</strong> video or audio piece: enable captions, skim for errors on names and jargon, and add a <strong>three-line text summary</strong> in the description or first comment if a full transcript is not realistic yet.</p>
<p>A transcript is the full text of everything said. A summary is the shortcut — three lines covering what someone needs to know if they cannot watch with sound. Start with the summary if a full transcript feels like too much this week.</p>
<p><strong>Do it on the platform you already use:</strong> YouTube auto-captions + edit. Instagram/TikTok caption tools. Zoom cloud recording → transcript export. Descript, CapCut, or native editors — use what you already touch, not a new stack.</p>
<h2>Part 2: Fix 5 — Links &amp; Flows That Don't Trap</h2>
<p><strong>The check:</strong> Does every link say where it goes? Can forms be completed without guessing?</p>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Lens</th><th>Pick one surface this week</th><th>What to change</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Small business</strong></td><td>"Book now," checkout, contact form, client portal</td><td>Replace "click here" with "Book a 20-minute session" / "Download the workbook." Visible labels on every field.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Chronically online</strong></td><td>Link tree, newsletter signup, DM → calendar flow</td><td>Descriptive link text; thumb-tap targets; say what happens after submit</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>Bad → better</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><p>"Click here" → "Register for the June workshop (Luma)"</p></li>
<li><p>"Learn more" → "Read the Neuroinclusive Design guide"</p></li>
<li><p>"Submit" alone on a long form → "Send my message to Amanda"</p></li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Your 15-minute action:</strong> Rewrite <strong>five</strong> "click here" or "learn more" links. Fix <strong>one</strong> form label that currently only makes sense if you already know your own business.</p>
<p><strong>Do it on the platform you already use:</strong> Carrd, Linktree, Beacons, Squarespace buttons, ConvertKit forms, Cal.com embeds — all allow custom button text and field labels.</p>
<h2>Part 3: Someone on the Other End — The Mystery Button</h2>
<p>A client taps <strong>Book now</strong> on your link-in-bio. They land on a generic scheduling page with no context — no service name, no price range, no "what to prepare." They close the tab. They tell themselves they will come back. They do not.</p>
<p>You had a booking link. You did not have <strong>explicit communication</strong> — Fix 5's lane.</p>
<p>Pair that with a tutorial video that only makes sense with sound on (Fix 4), and you have hosted the tools, not the human.</p>
<p><strong>Reflection prompt:</strong> Trace one path from social → booking → confirmation. Where is the first place you would get lost if you were tired?</p>
<p>I philosophize with other decolonizing neurodivergents about how our tech struggles reveal what we have internalized and what our brains actually need. June asked a simpler version of that question: can the person on the other end of your screen actually <strong>read</strong>, <strong>skim</strong>, <strong>see</strong>, <strong>hear</strong>, and <strong>follow</strong> what you built? Five fixes. Fifteen minutes each. That is the whole month in one sentence.</p>

<h2>Part 5: Month Close — Disarmed, Welcoming, Accessible</h2>
<p>If you have been reading since mid-June, Pope Leo XIV's <a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html"><em>Magnifica Humanitas</em></a> has been the wider frame — AI that must be "<strong>disarmed, welcoming and accessible</strong>," because merely regulating it is insufficient. Here is how the five fixes map to that charge at <strong>your</strong> scale:</p>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Leo's word</th><th>Your June fix</th><th>What it looks like in practice</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Accessible</strong></td><td>Fixes 1, 3, 4</td><td>Readable contrast; alt text; captions and transcripts</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Welcoming</strong></td><td>Fixes 2, 5</td><td>Scannable structure; links and forms that do not trap</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Disarmed</strong></td><td>All five, cumulatively</td><td>Refusing to let efficiency rhetoric outsource care to your audience</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p>One line I am carrying into July: accessible design accepts human limitation — tired eyes, slow connections, brains that skim, bodies that need captions. Captions and honest links help people who cannot be in the room. They do not replace the room. <strong>Show up when you can. Include everyone when you publish.</strong></p>
<p>Karen Hao's <a href="https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/743569/empire-of-ai-by-karen-hao/"><em>Empire of AI</em></a> names the colonial shape of data extraction at scale. The encyclical names the moral failure. Your checklist names what you can hold in mind at <strong>your</strong> scale when you publish.</p>
