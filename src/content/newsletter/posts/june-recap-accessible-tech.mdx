<p>You do not need to pass every row on every publish. The checklist is a memory aid for five kinds of complexity that are easy to forget when you are moving fast.</p>
<p>May's <strong>Mindful Automation</strong> hub gave us <strong>Y.O.U.R.</strong> — Reach asks who is on the other end. June added the layer underneath: when volume goes up, your obligation to preview goes up with it. Human eyes. Accessibility first. Then send.</p>
<h3>Disarmed, Welcoming, Accessible</h3>
<p>From mid-June on, Pope Leo XIV's encyclical <a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html"><em>Magnifica Humanitas</em></a> was the wider frame — AI that must be "<strong>disarmed, welcoming and accessible</strong>," because merely regulating it is insufficient.</p>
<p>Here is how the five fixes map to that charge at <strong>your</strong> scale:</p>
<table cellpadding="8" cellspacing="0" border="1" style="border-collapse:collapse;width:100%;font-size:15px;">
<thead><tr><th>Leo's word</th><th>Your June fix</th><th>What it looks like in practice</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Accessible</strong></td><td>Fixes 1, 3, 4</td><td>Readable contrast; alt text; captions and transcripts</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Welcoming</strong></td><td>Fixes 2, 5</td><td>Scannable structure; links and forms that do not trap</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Disarmed</strong></td><td>All five, cumulatively</td><td>Refusing to let efficiency rhetoric outsource care to your audience</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p>Leo writes that <a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html">technology is never neutral</a> — it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, and use it. Communication works the same way. An image without description is a choice about who counts as your audience. A newsletter laid out by an agent nobody previewed on a phone is a choice too — Week 1 opened on exactly that failure mode.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html"><em>Magnifica Humanitas</em></a> landed in a complicated room. <a href="https://www.newyorker.com/news/the-lede/what-pope-leo-xiv-said-about-ai">Jill Lepore reports</a> that Anthropic co-founder Chris Olah appeared on the dais beside Leo at the release — unprecedented, and freighted with symbolism. Leo urges the public to push back against executives who resist restrictions. He also writes that <strong>a more moral AI is not enough if that morality is determined by a few.</strong> We do not have "ethical AI" in any finished sense — we have products, incentives, and pattern matchers.</p>
<p>Karen Hao's <a href="https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/743569/empire-of-ai-by-karen-hao/"><em>Empire of AI</em></a> names the colonial shape of data extraction at scale. The encyclical names the moral failure. Your checklist names what you can hold in mind at <strong>your</strong> scale when you publish.</p>
<p>Accessible design accepts human limitation — tired eyes, slow connections, brains that skim, bodies that need captions. Alt text and honest links help people who cannot be in the room. They do not replace the room. <strong>Show up when you can. Include everyone when you publish.</strong></p>
<p>I philosophize with other decolonizing neurodivergents about how our tech struggles reveal what we have internalized and what our brains actually need. June asked a simpler version of that question: can the person on the other end of your screen actually use what you built?</p>
<h3>What I Did With the Extra Bandwidth</h3>
<p>I spent June testing a theory: if I protect my own bandwidth, the work I choose to ship stays honest.</p>
<p>The bandwidth part came first. I am <strong>officially not on the job hunt right now.</strong> I <strong>canceled events</strong> I had planned but never announced — last-minute life stuff clarified priorities fast. The question that helped was <em>what would I rather not worry about?</em> One answer was a make-believe obligation I had invented for myself. Delete it. Move on.</p>
<p>What filled the room surprised me. I had fallen out of reading for so long that finishing one book felt like a stretch. In June I finished <strong>four</strong>. I kept starting something new on a weekend and finishing it before the week picked back up — which, for me, is the clearest sign I am not running on fumes.</p>
<p>When I tap into pure enjoyment, I come back to my work with more certainty. June reminded me that <strong>output can be a diamond dot painting or a silly little crochet thing</strong> — it does not have to be helpful to future-you every single time. It can just be cute and bring joy.</p>
<p>That is how a month ships at newsletter-and-checklist pace instead of sprint-video pace. The work stayed honest because the rest did too. I am logging reads on <a href="https://fable.co/fabler/pythoness-programmer-506807855588">Fable</a> if you want to follow along.</p>
